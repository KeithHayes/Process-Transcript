With each turn of page, she found herself immersed deeper into the lives of characters who were once strangers but now felt familiar; their joys and sorrows resonating deeply within her own heart. As dusk began to fall outside, casting long shadows across the room, she closed the book, marked her place, and reached for her cup of chamomile-infused tea—and rest. Turning back to her anthology, she selected a different story. This one described an early morning in a busy city, horse-drawn carriages rattling over cobblestone streets, merchants hawking their wares at street stalls, and the clamor of voices in unfamiliar tongues. As Alice read, she imagined herself there; she could almost hear the clip-clop of hooves and feel the rough stone underfoot, the weight of her satchel, and stepping out onto the back porch to breathe beneath a sky of stars when she finally stood the teacup empty—the anthology closed, and the quiet settled deeply over the room. Alice felt a gentle contentment, gratitude, even for the stories—yes, and for the world beyond them, for the tactile living reality she inhabited, and so, at the close of day, she gave thanks for words, for solitude, and for the small wonders that attended each ordinary moment.
